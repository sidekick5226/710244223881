Crosley Voyager CR8017A-BK

Dissection Report & Technical Analysis

A comprehensive engineering analysis of a modern portable turntable combining mechanical, analog, and digital subsystems in one compact consumer embedded system.

1

Introduction

I am a senior computer engineering student interested in signal processing and embedded systems. For this project, I chose a Crosley briefcase-style record player (Cruiser-type portable turntable) because it combines mechanical, analog, and digital subsystems in one small product. The device includes a belt-driven motor, a ceramic phono cartridge, integrated audio amplification, and (in my model) Bluetooth connectivity, making it a compact example of a modern consumer embedded system.

2

Product Identification & History

2.1 Product Identification and History

The dissected device is a Crosley Voyager portable turntable, model CR8017A-BK, finished in black. It is a three-speed (33⅓, 45, and 78 RPM) belt-drive record player with built-in stereo speakers, Bluetooth audio input, adjustable pitch control, RCA line-out, AUX-in, and a headphone jack. Crosley Radio markets the Voyager line as an entry-level, suitcase-style record player that blends a vintage aesthetic with modern features such as Bluetooth streaming.

The Voyager family (including CR8017A color variants like AM, BR, DU, FL, GY, SA, WB, etc.) appears in online catalogs starting around 2021, positioned as a step up from the smaller "Cruiser" series, with a slightly larger cabinet and front-firing speaker grille.

Timing Diagrams

Waveform 1: Motor Voltage Step Response

This shows the voltage at the motor terminal when the platter motor is enabled. The voltage steps from 0 V to approximately 9 V. Because the motor is an inductive load driven from a low-impedance DC supply, the step exhibits a brief damped ringing at about 60 kHz (measured from the oscillation period ≈ 17 µs). The waveform settles to its final value in tens of microseconds, after which the motor sees a nearly constant DC voltage while it spins up mechanically.

Waveform 2: PHONO/BT Mode Control Transition

The PHONO/BT mode control line transitions from 0 V to ~2.6 V with a rise time of ~40 µs when the user switches to Bluetooth.

Product Reassembly

The turntable was successfully reassembled following reverse order of disassembly. All components were reconnected, and the unit was tested for functionality. The device powers on correctly, the motor spins at the appropriate speeds (33⅓, 45, and 78 RPM), and audio output functions properly through both built-in speakers and auxiliary outputs. Bluetooth connectivity was verified and operates as expected.

7

Lessons Learned

Design for Cost

This dissection clearly demonstrates design-for-cost principles in consumer electronics. The Crosley Voyager achieves its low retail price point through several strategic design decisions:

  • • Use of commodity components (generic ceramic cartridge, basic belt-drive motor)
  • • Minimal BOM with shared circuit boards across product variants
  • • Simple mechanical assembly (plywood cabinet, basic hinges and latches)
  • • Integration of multiple functions on single PCB (power, audio, Bluetooth)

As an engineering student focused on embedded systems, this project reinforced the importance of understanding the entire product ecosystem—from mechanical design to signal processing to manufacturing cost constraints. Real-world products must balance technical performance with economic viability, and the Crosley Voyager exemplifies that balance in the consumer audio market.
